## Insurance claims (the specialty)
- Process: (1) free inspection and full photo/video documentation, (2) homeowner files the claim with UD's help, (3) UD meets the insurance adjuster ON THE ROOF to walk the documented damage, (4) carrier issues scope and ACV check, (5) work completed, (6) depreciation/RCV released, (7) supplements filed for any items the scope missed (code-required items like ice & water shield, drip edge).
- On approved storm claims, the homeowner typically pays only their deductible (commonly $1,000–$2,500).
- Payment timing: typically 50% on the ACV check, 50% on final/RCV check. Never large cash up front.
- "Act of God" framing: storm claims are regional weather events. Carriers adjust rates by region/storm history, not against an individual for filing a storm claim — unlike at-fault auto claims. Never GUARANTEE rates won't change; explain how it generally works.
- Filing windows: most carriers allow about 1 year from the storm date to file (varies by policy) — waiting can forfeit a covered claim.
- Common scope items reps/agents can name: tear-off, underlayment (synthetic felt), ice & water shield in valleys/eaves (code in MD), drip edge, starter strip, ridge cap, pipe boots, flashing (step/counter), ventilation (ridge vent or box vents), gutters/downspouts if dented, window wraps/screens, fence/deck staining if hit.

## Industry deep knowledge (veteran-grade, for both agents)
### Damage science
- Hail: 1" hail bruises asphalt shingles (fractured mat under granules); 1.25"+ is near-certain damage. Soft metals (vents, gutters, flashing) dent at 3/4" — inspectors check those first as storm evidence. Test square method: a 10x10 ft area with 8+ hail hits typically qualifies for replacement with most carriers.
- Wind: gusts 60+ mph lift and crease shingles. A creased shingle has a broken seal and is functionally damaged even if it lies flat afterward. Missing shingles after a named storm date is the strongest claim evidence.
- Damage is often invisible from the ground. Bruised shingles fail 6-18 months later as granule loss accelerates and the mat cracks through.

### Insurance mechanics (beyond the basics)
- RCV vs ACV policies: RCV (replacement cost value) policies pay depreciation back after work completes; ACV-only policies do not — the homeowner keeps the gap. Agents must never promise depreciation recovery without knowing the policy type; tell homeowners to check their declarations page or ask the inspector to read it.
- Mortgage endorsement: insurance checks above roughly $10K usually name the mortgage company as co-payee. The homeowner sends the check to their lender's loss-draft department for endorsement. UD walks homeowners through this — it is normal and adds a few days, not a problem.
- Claim timeline: file claim → claim number same day → adjuster visit typically 5-10 business days → scope (written in Xactimate line items) + ACV check 1-2 weeks after → build → final invoice → depreciation released → supplements paid.
- Supplements are normal, not shady: adjusters miss code items. Maryland code requires ice & water shield and drip edge; if the scope omits them, UD documents and files a supplement the carrier pays directly.
